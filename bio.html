Born in Sacramento the son of a horse trainer, the first half my childhood was spent on ranches riding horses and chasing cows. The second half, after my father switched careers, was spent near Placerville in the foothills of the Sierra Nevada. I began attending Cedar Springs Waldorf School in the second grade, and eventually went to Sacramento Waldorf for high school. Waldorf education is nondenominational and emphasizes holistic child development and a connection with the natural world. Kids are allowed to dig holes and climb trees, they learn Egyptian, Norse, Greek, and Roman mythology, and explore science through wonder. Music and art are required and emphasized. In short, it lets kids be kids.

Not many people attend four different colleges to complete their undergraduate degree. From Redlands to Davis, I have experienced nearly every possible form of US undergraduate education. I didn’t intend to fragment my undergraduate experience, it just turned out that way because I wasn’t afraid to follow my aspirations. Originally a chemistry major, the academic demands of school conflicted with my, at the time very serious, baseball ambitions. After refusing to compromise my school work, I found the athletic environment at my first university to be inhospitable. After two years, I began to search elsewhere.

This led me to Chico State, where the coach liked my abilities and gave me a spot on the team as a catcher. I found out two weeks before school was to start that I would not be admitted because I hadn’t taken a required lower division English course (which I hadn’t needed previously). I got out of my lease and ended up at Folsom Lake College. I made the team but left after a semester because the coach was abusive.

I was then recruited by Santa Rosa Junior College. During that next semester in Santa Rosa I struggled with what I wanted out of life. I had spent so much of it focused on baseball that I could hardly imagine life without it. However, by that point I had become severely disillusioned with collegiate baseball programs and was beginning to realize that it wasn’t going to get any better. Coaches’ egos and insecurities caused them to treat us as puppets rather than pupils, and my love for the game was not strong enough to keep me there. So, following my heart, I quit and enrolled in courses that would allow me to transfer to UC Davis.

Energized by my newfound direction, I dove deeply into my studies and transferred into civil engineering. I chose the program because it allowed me to study problems in the environment with a focus on developing solutions and because I knew that an engineering degree would provide training in mathematics, problem solving, and critical thinking that would be useful in any career.

My experiences have taught me that following your passion yields good results, even if those results weren’t originally intended. At times, it has been tough to keep going; this unconventional educational path has required patience, planning, and perseverance. I am driven by my conviction that the way to find satisfaction in life is to do beneficial work and to strive to make the world a better place. Working on environmental problems via environmental engineering is the way I choose to do this, and it is well-suited to the task, as it deals with the basic necessities of of life and develops practical, science-based solutions.
